Methodology, caveats, levers

Scores run from −50 to +50, centered on zero. Zero is the neutral point: regulation only to the extent of the pre-AI status quo on privacy and employment law. Positive values indicate increasing pro-safety posture — +10 to +20 for audit-only regimes; +20 to +30 for federal funding of US CAISI and dedicated Capitol Hill committees with high-risk use-case exclusions; +30 to +40 for a dedicated agency with mandatory evaluations and enforcement; +40 to +50 for compute/data-center moratoria with massive penalties, nationalization, and complete bans on frontier models above 10²⁶ FLOPs. Negative values indicate increasing pro-acceleration posture — −10 to −20 for voting down AI regulations; −20 to −30 for federal preemption that supersedes stricter state AI laws; −30 to −40 for active moratoria on state regulation and direct acceleration votes; −40 to −50 for direct subsidies targeting superintelligence and advocacy for embedding AI in autonomous weapons, mass surveillance, or wholesale government labor replacement.

Time decay. Each finding is weighted so that its contribution to the distribution decays linearly toward zero (neutral) over ten years. A 2025 statement counts at full weight; a 2020 statement counts at roughly half weight; a 2015 statement counts for nothing — it is fully decayed to neutral. The decay is applied inside the Opus 4.5 grading step, not in post-processing, so the distribution itself reflects the time-weighted record.

Caveats. This is a probabilistic model built on public record — floor statements, votes, letters, interviews, bill sponsorship. It is not a predictor of a specific bill's outcome. It does not account for logrolling, caucus discipline, or committee procedure. Members with thin public records on AI default toward zero with wide uncertainty; do not confuse a wide distribution with a considered position. Sonnet 4.5 and Opus 4.5 can err; grading is anchored in short summaries which the user can inspect and override.

Levers for change. The shape of the distribution suggests where a single bill dies: look at the density of members whose 90% CI crosses the zero line. Those are the persuadable middle. A regulation clearing +10 in the Senate needs the mass above +5 to hold; a moratorium clearing −30 needs mass below −5. The yellow zero-row separator in the sorted table makes this structural boundary unmistakable. Read the curves, not the headline.
